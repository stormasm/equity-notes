
### what does average duration mean in bond funds

In bond funds, average duration measures how sensitive the fund's price is to changes in interest rates, expressed in years. For every 1% rise in interest rates, a bond fund's value will generally fall by a percentage equal to its duration, and vice versa.

A fund's average duration is a weighted average of the durations of all the individual bonds it holds. It accounts for both the remaining time until those bonds mature and the regular interest payments they make.

Here is how you can interpret different duration lengths:

- Short duration (1-3 years): Lower interest rate risk; the fund's price will not fluctuate wildly when rates change.
- Intermediate duration (3-6 years): A moderate balance between risk and potential return.
- Long duration (6+ years): Higher sensitivity to rate changes, making the fund more volatile but potentially offering higher yields.

For example, if a bond fund has an average duration of 5 years, its price will drop by approximately 5% if interest rates increase by 1%
